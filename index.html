02 · The loop

Detection tells you what happened. Learning changes what happens next.

An Apple Watch can collect data. A chatbot can give advice. A journal app can record what happened. None of them turn tonight's real meltdown into a better tomorrow. Kidence is a parent learning system — detect → coach the parent → the parent gets better → the child gets better. The hardware's job is simply to make that learning automatic.

i. Monitor — zero effort

A wearable that watches quietly

A lightweight on‑wrist model reads HR, EDA, motion and skin temperature against your child's own baseline, tracking a simple arousal & regulation state — Calm, Elevated, High, Recovering. Nothing to do on an ordinary day.

ii. Micro‑coach — 3 seconds

One move, and the words to say

When arousal climbs, you get exactly one suggestion — “Crouch down. Say: you're really disappointed, I'm right here” — ranked by what has actually worked for your child. No menus, no typing, ignorable without penalty.

iii. Review — 90 seconds

Tell the story once

After recovery, just talk: what happened, what you tried, how they reacted. Kidence turns it into structured evidence — triggers, feelings, what helped. If your child is nearby, they pick their own feeling card and open the sticker pack together with you.

iv. Practice — 60 seconds

Practice Together: the AI plays your child

In the evening, one short rep built from today's real moment. The AI plays your child in character; you answer out loud; a coach scores the exchange and hands you a pocket plan. Then a twist — same trigger, new situation — so the skill generalizes.

A day with Kidence: 3 seconds during the storm · 90 seconds after · 60 seconds in the evening. Everything else is passive. Under the hood, a bounded, clinician‑reviewable action library is re‑ranked per child by a learning policy — alert #50 is not alert #1.

03 · Online learning

Continual learning, not snapshot training.

Affect is non‑stationary. The triggers, baselines, and effective responses for any given person drift across days, contexts, and developmental stages. A model trained once and frozen will degrade. Kidence's personalization model updates online with each new event and each piece of feedback, in the spirit of continual reinforcement learning — reweighting predictions and intervention rankings as evidence accumulates.

What updates online
  • →Per‑user alert threshold and lead‑time calibration.
  • →Intervention policy — which response, in which context, for this child.
  • →The parent's playbook: trigger categories (fairness, transitions, overwhelm) learned from every review, so practice scenarios train the skill, not the script.
  • →Contextual priors: time of day, recent sleep, prior events.
What stays fixed
  • →The base anomaly detector ships as a population‑trained edge model, updated only by deliberate OTA release.
  • →Clinician‑approved intervention candidates remain bounded; the policy chooses among them, it does not generate them.
  • →Safety constraints and escalation rules are not learned.

“The world is non‑stationary; a learning system must therefore keep learning.”

— framing inspired by recent work on continual reinforcement learning. Technical details in our manuscript (under review).

04 · Families

Built for family life. Rooted in clinical research.

The physiological precursors of a tantrum, a meltdown, and an anxious spiral overlap more than the labels suggest. We built the predictive core in clinical contexts — and we're bringing it to the moments families actually live.

04.1 · Early warning · Ages 3–12

A cue before the boil‑over

Tantrums, meltdowns, bedtime battles, screen‑time transitions, sibling conflict. The wearable notices the physiological build‑up before behavior turns, and quietly cues the parent while the moment is still workable.

04.2 · Parent coaching · In the moment & between moments

What to say, what to do — and a place to rehearse it

During the storm: one concrete, personalized suggestion — the words and moves most likely to work for this child, learned from what has actually helped before. Between storms: Practice Together — the AI plays your child in a scenario built from your real week, you rehearse your response out loud, and a coach sharpens it. Sixty seconds, never homework. You don't learn a new play during the game; you practice it the night before.

04.3 · Self‑regulation · With your child

Helping kids understand their feelings

Each caught moment becomes a gentle teaching opportunity: noticing the feeling, naming it, and choosing what to do with it. Over time the goal is for the early cue to become the child's own awareness — and for moods to grow more stable.

04.4 · Clinical research

The same predictive substrate, in clinical contexts

Our research continues with clinical partners across autism (pre‑meltdown alerts for caregivers and BCBAs), panic disorder, and PTSD — the populations where we first validated that affective escalation announces itself physiologically before it surfaces.
